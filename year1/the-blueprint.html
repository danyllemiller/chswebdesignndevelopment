Chapter 3 Overview: Level Up: Knowing Your User

Welcome to the most critical stage of the design process. Before you write a single line of code, pick a color palette, or choose a font, you must answer one fundamental question: Who is this for?

In the professional world, we don't build websites based on our own opinions. We build them based on User Experience (UX) Design. UX is the process of making things work well for a specific person. It is the bridge between a "pretty" website and a "useful" website.

Business owners in Carson City don't just want a site; they want a tool that helps their specific customers—whether they are tourists visiting the Mint or locals looking for a mechanic on Winnie Ln. By mastering research, you ensure your agency delivers results, not just code.

The Most Common Mistake

What's the #1 mistake new developers make? They build a website for themselves.

They use the colors they like. They add the features they think are cool. They organize the navigation based on what they think is logical. But unless you are the only person who will ever visit your site, this is a recipe for disaster.

Big secret: You are not your user.

You might be a 15-year-old gamer who loves high-contrast neon. Your user might be a 65-year-old grandma looking for a knitting pattern. She doesn't want flashing animations; she wants big, readable text and a clear "Print Recipe" button.

If you don't know who you're building for, you will fail. UX Design is about empathy—the ability to see the world through someone else's eyes and build something that solves their specific needs.

Phase 1: The Research (How to Find "The User")

You can't build a useful profile based on a guess. You need hard data. In professional web design, we rely on two distinct types of research to build our plan for a site like PixelPlay (our retro-gaming community).

Research Type Qualitative (The "Why") Quantitative (The "What" & "How Many")
Goal To understand why users behave a certain way. Explores feelings and pain points. To measure what users do. Gathers statistical data to validate hypotheses.
Sample Size Small (5-8 people is often enough to find patterns) Large (100+ responses to be statistically useful)
Methods User Interviews, Field Studies, Usability Tests Surveys, A/B Tests, Web Analytics
Question Type Open-ended: "Walk me through your day..." Closed-ended: "How many times a week do you..."
Data Output Quotes, observations, insights, and stories. Charts, graphs, percentages, and statistics.

Digging for the "Why" and Proving the "What"

User Interviews: Conversations with people representing your target audience. We look for Pain Points. If a user says, "I hate digging through a 50-page forum just to find one answer," they've told you exactly what to fix.

Pro Tip: Don't "Lead the Witness." Instead of "Don't you hate GameFAQs?", ask "Walk me through the last time you used it. What was that like?"

Surveys: Numbers to back up your stories. If 80% of 200 responses say they use their Phone while playing on a Desktop/TV, your site must be mobile-first.

We synthesize (combine) all that research data into one fictional character: your User Persona. It is a tool used to build empathy. Instead of "I like blue," we ask "Would Ryan find this button easy to see?"

The Empathy Map: Getting Inside Their Head

SAYS (Interviews)THINKS (Internal Monologue)
"I can never find guides that aren't full of ads." "Why does every site look like it was made in 1999?"
DOES (Behaviors)FEELS (Emotional state)
Takes a picture of high score screen with a phone. Frustrated when digging; Proud of achievements.

Because Ryan THINKS "why is this so ugly," our site must be clean. Because he DOES use his phone while playing, our pages must be mobile-responsive.

Phase 4: The Features (User Stories & MoSCoW)

We write User Stories to keep focus on benefits: "As a [type of user], I want to [do a thing] so that I can [achieve a goal]."

"As a community member, I want to submit my high score with a screenshot so that I can get on the leaderboard."

MMust HaveThe site is useless without this. (The "M" in MVP).

The Secret Sauce: Acceptance Criteria

GIVEN: I am on the homepage
WHEN: I type "Earthbound"
THEN: Result appears.
AND WHEN: I click result
THEN: I see the game page.

Phase 5: The Workflow (The "ADDIE" Model)

A professional build follows the ADDIE Model, a 5-step cycle used to build everything from websites to video games.

  • A - Analyze: User research, Persona, User Stories (Phase 1-4).
  • D - Design: Creating the Sitemap and Wireframes (Phase 5).
  • D - Develop: Actually writing the code (HTML, CSS, JS).
  • I - Implement: Launching the site on a web host.
  • E - Evaluate: Post-launch data analysis and user feedback.

The Sitemap (The "Map")

A Sitemap is a simple, top-down diagram that shows all the pages on your website and how they connect. It starts with the Homepage (index.html) at the top.

This turns your abstract "User Stories" into a concrete list of HTML files you need to build.

The Wireframe (The "Blueprint")

A Wireframe is a black-and-white blueprint for a single page. It has NO colors, NO fonts, and NO real images. It only focuses on layout, hierarchy, and priority.

Levels of "Fidelity" (Detail)
  • Low-Fidelity (Lo-Fi): Fast paper sketches for rapid ideation.
  • Mid-Fidelity (Mid-Fi): Clean, digital black-and-white version in Figma.
  • High-Fidelity (Hi-Fi): Pixel-perfect mockup ready for coding.
Why no color? Because if you use color, you'll waste 20 minutes talking about blue instead of what really matters: "Is the button in the right place?"

Planning UI with "Atomic Design"

We build our code like LEGOs, breaking UIs down into smallest parts:

Atoms
Basic HTML tags: <button>, <input>, <label>.
Molecules
Groups like a Search Form (Label + Input + Button).
Organisms
Complex sections like a Site Header or Game Grid.

Understanding "How" Users Read

F-Pattern:

Used for text-heavy pages (Blogs). Users scan top, then down the left, then across again briefly.

Z-Pattern:

Used for simple, visual Homepages. Users scan top-left to top-right, diagonal down, then bottom-right.

Helping Google Find You (SEO)

SEO (Search Engine Optimization) is the science of ranking #1. Focus on the "Holy Trinity":

The <title> Tag Tells Google the main topic.

Good: <title>Super Metroid Walkthrough | PixelPlay</title>
<meta description> Your "ad" snippet.

Good: Need a Super Metroid guide? See our maps and secrets...
The <h1> Tag The visible headline. You should only have ONE per page so search engines know exactly what the page is about.

Keywords: Use Long-Tail Keywords (e.g., "best SNES RPGs with time travel") to capture users with specific intent.
