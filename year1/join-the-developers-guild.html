The Founding & The Big Misconception

People use "Internet" and "Web" as the same thing all the time, but they're not. Think of the Internet as the physical highway system (like Hwy 395 connecting our towns) and the Web as the collection of digital destinations you can visit.

The Internet

is the physical "stuff." It's the entire road system. It's the network of roads, highways, and bridges (the giant cables under the ocean, the Wi-Fi routers, and servers) that connect all the cities and towns in the world. It's the physical infrastructure.

The World Wide Web (the Web)

is the information that travels on the internet. It's a system of interconnected documents and applications we access through the Internet. It's the destinations you visit, like stores and restaurants (websites, apps, and video streams).

The Web is a service that runs on the Internet. It could not exist without the Internet, but the Internet can exist without the Web (for things like email or some online gaming).

The Internet vs. The Intranet: Public Highway vs. Private Road

While the Internet is a massive, public network, many organizations use a smaller, private version called an intranet. An intranet is a restricted, internal network only for members of a specific group, like a company's employees (for example, the internal secure networks used by staff at the Nevada State Capitol or Carson Tahoe Hospital). If the Internet is the public highway system, an intranet is a private, gated road.

A Super-Quick History

This all started in the 1960s as ARPANET, a U.S. government project to connect computers at research labs so they could share information. It was built to be decentralized, a big fear during the Cold War.

How the Web Was Born

The Developer community has a history. Tim Berners-Lee is credited with inventing the World Wide Web in 1989 while working as a physicist at CERN, a research lab in Switzerland. He had a big problem: scientists had tons of important research, but it was all locked away on different computers that couldn't easily talk to each other. So, he invented a system that would let them share documents by linking them together. This system was the World Wide Web. He made the very first website in 1990, and it was a simple page explaining the project. He didn't just invent the rules; he created the very first web browser, called WorldWideWeb.

Berners-Lee pioneered the "Open Web" philosophy: the idea that the web should be a public resource that is open and accessible to all, not controlled by one company. The web used to be "Read-Only." The transition from that original library-style web to the social, interactive, and user-generated web we use today is known as Web 2.0.

The Rules of the Road: Meet the W3C

Imagine you're building with Lego. What if every company had its own unique Lego block that didn't fit with anyone else's? That would be a total mess! The World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) is like the international committee that makes sure all the Lego bricks fit together. They are the rule-makers who decide on the standards for technologies like HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, so that websites and browsers can all talk to each other. This is why you can view the same website on a Chrome browser on your laptop and a Safari browser on your phone without things breaking.

How You "Get" a Website (The "Where")

This is the most important concept to understand. Every interaction on the Web is powered by a client-server relationship. When you type a website's address into your web browser (the client), your request is sent across the Internet to a powerful computer called a server, where the website's files are stored. The server finds those files (webpages) and sends them back to your browser, which displays the page on your screen. This constant back-and-forth of request and response is the engine that makes the Web possible.

The Internet's Address Book

But wait. How does your browser find that specific server among the billions of devices on the internet? It can't just "drive around" looking for it. It needs a precise address. This all works thanks to a three-part system: IP addresses, Domain Names, and the DNS.

IP Address: The "GPS Coordinate"

Every device on the internet—your phone, your laptop, and every web server—has a unique address called an IP Address (Internet Protocol Address). Think of it as the literal GPS coordinate for a location. It's a string of numbers like 172.217.14.228 (an IPv4 address) or 2001:0db8:85a3:0000:0000:8a2e:0370:7334 (a newer IPv6 address). Computers love these numbers. But humans are terrible at remembering them.

Domain Name: The "Street Address"

Because we can't remember those numbers, we invented Domain Names. A domain name (like google.com or pixelpaws.com) is a human-friendly, memorable nickname for an IP address. It's like putting the name "The White House" on the building at 1600 Pennsylvania Avenue.

You don't "buy" a domain; you lease or register it from a Domain Registrar (like GoDaddy or Namecheap). These are ICANN-accredited companies authorized to sell domain registrations. They are all managed by a global non-profit called ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers), which is like the "government" that sets the rules for the entire system.

DNS: The "Phone Book" Operator

So, what's the glue that connects the domain name you type (google.com) to the IP address the computer needs (172.217.14.228)? A magical thing called the DNS (Domain Name System).

It's the internet's giant "phone book." When you type google.com, a multi-step process happens in milliseconds:

  1. Your browser first asks a Recursive Resolver (your ISP): "Where is google.com?"
  2. The Resolver asks a Root Server: "Where can I find a .com server?"
  3. The Root Server replies: "I don't know google.com, but the TLD Server for .com is over there."
  4. The Resolver asks the .com TLD Server: "Where is google.com?"
  5. The TLD Server replies: "I know the official Authoritative Nameservers for google.com. Go ask them."
  6. The Resolver asks Google's Authoritative Nameserver: "What is the IP address for google.com?"
  7. The Nameserver says: "I've got this! The IP address is 172.217.14.228."
  8. Your Resolver caches this answer and tells your browser. Your browser can now talk directly to the server.
Understanding the DNS Lookup Process

When you type a URL into your browser, a complex background process happens in a matter of milliseconds to find the correct server. Your browser first checks its own cache, and if it doesn't know the IP address, it asks your Internet Service Provider's Recursive Resolver.

The Resolver acts like a digital detective. First, it asks the Root Server (the top of the internet hierarchy). The Root Server doesn't know the exact address, but it directs the Resolver to the correct TLD (Top-Level Domain) Server (like the specific server that handles all .com or .org addresses).

The TLD Server then points the Resolver to the Authoritative Nameserver—the final boss that holds the official record for that specific website. The Authoritative server hands over the exact IP address, the Resolver passes it back to your browser, and your browser finally connects directly to the web server to load the page!

DNS Propagation: Why Changes Take Time

When you update a DNS record — like pointing your domain to a new server after switching hosts — the change doesn't instantly reach every device on the internet. Each record has a TTL (Time to Live), a number in seconds that tells DNS resolvers how long to cache the answer before re-checking. A TTL of 3600 means servers worldwide may keep the old answer for up to one hour. Full global propagation can take anywhere from minutes to 48 hours. This is why developers lower their TTL values days before a planned migration.

Public DNS: Faster & More Private

By default your computer uses your ISP's DNS server. You can change it to a faster, more private alternative. Cloudflare (1.1.1.1) is consistently the fastest public resolver and has a strict no-log policy. Google (8.8.8.8) is the world's most widely used. OpenDNS offers family filtering. Switching your home or school router's DNS takes about 60 seconds and costs nothing.

The "S" in HTTPS: Your Digital Bodyguard

Never enter a password on a site that is not https://. That "s" proof the site is using an SSL Certificate to create a secure, encrypted connection. Think of it like a secret agent handshake:

  1. Your browser asks the server for ID.
  2. Server shows its SSL Certificate (Public Key).
  3. Browser verifies it, creates a secret Session Key, and locks it in the Public Key "mailbox."
  4. Only the server has the Private Key to open the mailbox and get the secret code. Handshake complete.
The TLS Handshake at a Glance — How HTTPS Protects You

2
Server Certificate — The server responds with its SSL/TLS certificate, signed by a trusted Certificate Authority (CA) like DigiCert or Let's Encrypt, proving identity.
3
Key Exchange — Your browser generates a random session key and encrypts it using the server's public key. Only the server's private key can decrypt it.
4
Encrypted Channel Active — Both sides share the session key. All data now flows encrypted. The padlock icon appears. Entire handshake: under 100ms.

If your domain name is the street address, then hosting is the actual plot of land where your building sits. Without hosting, your website exists only on your local computer and nobody can see it.

What to Look For in a Hosting Plan

When evaluating hosting, these are the numbers that matter:

  • Storage (Disk Space): How many GB of files — HTML, images, video, databases — you can store on the server.
  • Bandwidth: Total data transferred to visitors per month. A 2 MB page viewed 10,000 times uses 20 GB of bandwidth.
  • Uptime SLA: 99.9% uptime = ~8.7 hours of downtime per year. 99.99% = ~52 minutes. This is a contractual promise in the Service Level Agreement.
  • Managed vs. Unmanaged: Managed means the provider handles security updates and maintenance. Unmanaged means lower cost but full technical responsibility on you.
  • Control Panel: Most shared hosts provide cPanel or Plesk — graphical dashboards for managing files, databases, email, and domains without using the command line.

Speed Boost: CDNs (Content Delivery Networks)

A CDN (Content Delivery Network) is a global network of servers placed in cities worldwide called edge servers. When a user requests your site, instead of fetching files from your origin server (which might be in Virginia), they download them from the nearest edge location — Los Angeles, London, or Tokyo. Static assets like images, CSS, fonts, and JavaScript are cached at these edges. The result: dramatically faster load times globally, reduced load on your origin server, and built-in DDoS protection. Cloudflare is the most widely used CDN — its basic tier is completely free.

The fundamental building blocks every designer uses — in web design, graphic design, photography, and beyond.

  • Line: Creates paths, outlines, and boundaries. Lines guide the eye and suggest movement — horizontal lines feel calm, diagonal lines feel energetic.
  • Shape: Two-dimensional areas — geometric (circles, squares, triangles) or organic (irregular, freeform). Shapes define content regions and buttons.
  • Form: Gives the illusion of three dimensions through shading, gradients, or perspective. Shadows under cards give depth to flat design.
  • Space (White Space): The empty area around and between elements. Space is not wasted — it gives content room to breathe and helps users focus.
  • Texture: The simulated feel of a surface — grainy backgrounds, fabric overlays, or smooth glass effects. Used sparingly to add visual interest.
  • Value: The lightness or darkness of a color. High contrast (dark on light) creates emphasis; low contrast creates subtlety and hierarchy.
  • Color: The most emotionally powerful element — hue (the color itself), saturation (intensity), and brightness (lightness). Covered in Color Theory below.
Principles of Design

The rules designers follow when arranging the elements of art to create an effective visual layout.

  • Balance: Visual weight distributed evenly across the layout — symmetric (mirrored left/right) or asymmetric (different elements that still feel stable).
  • Contrast: Differences that make elements stand out — dark vs. light, large vs. small, bold vs. thin. The most important design tool for readability.
  • Emphasis (Focal Point): Drawing the eye to the most important element first — a large hero image, a brightly colored call-to-action button, or a bold headline.
  • Movement: Guiding the viewer's eye through the layout in a deliberate order — achieved with lines, arrows, size progression, or reading patterns (see F/Z patterns below).
  • Repetition / Pattern: Repeating visual elements (colors, shapes, spacing) creates consistency and rhythm. Every website uses repetition in its navigation, card components, and buttons.
  • Proportion: The size relationship between elements — a large H1 heading over smaller body text signals importance. Proportion also means keeping components correctly sized relative to each other.
  • Unity: Everything on the page feels like it belongs together — consistent color palette, font family, spacing, and visual style. Unity is the difference between a professional site and a chaotic one.
User-Centric Design Principles

Beyond visual beauty, web design must serve real people navigating real tasks.

  • Usability: How quickly and easily can a first-time visitor find what they need? Measured through user testing, click tracking, and heatmaps.
  • Simplicity & White Space: Remove anything that doesn't serve a purpose. White space is not empty space — it directs attention and reduces cognitive load.
  • Accessibility (a11y): Alt text for images, sufficient color contrast (4.5:1 ratio minimum), keyboard navigation, and screen reader support. Required by law for government and education sites.
  • Visual Hierarchy: The most important content is the largest, boldest, and highest on the page. A clear hierarchy lets users scan and understand a page in seconds.
  • Typography: Font choice, size hierarchy (H1 → H2 → body → caption), line height (leading), and letter spacing (kerning/tracking). Covered in depth below.
  • Color Psychology: Colors trigger emotions — blues feel trustworthy (banks, healthcare), greens feel natural or safe (eco brands), reds feel urgent or exciting (sales, alerts).
  • Alignment, Balance & Contrast: Aligned elements feel intentional; misaligned elements feel careless. Contrast makes critical information impossible to miss.
Color Theory Basics

Color is the first thing visitors feel before they consciously process anything else on the page.

  • Primary Colors: Red, Yellow, Blue — the foundation of all other colors on the traditional color wheel.
  • Secondary Colors: Orange, Green, Violet — created by mixing two adjacent primaries.
  • Complementary Colors: Opposites on the color wheel (e.g., red & green, blue & orange). High contrast — great for emphasis and call-to-action buttons.
  • Analogous Colors: Neighbors on the wheel (e.g., blue, blue-green, green). Harmonious and easy on the eye — used for backgrounds and gradients.
  • Warm vs. Cool: Reds, oranges, and yellows feel urgent, energetic, or friendly. Blues, greens, and purples feel calm, trustworthy, or professional.
  • The 60-30-10 Rule: 60% dominant color (backgrounds, large sections), 30% secondary color (cards, sidebars), 10% accent (buttons, highlights). Borrowed from interior design — it works everywhere.
  • WCAG Contrast Standard: Normal text must achieve a 4.5:1 contrast ratio against its background. Large text (18pt+) needs 3:1. Use WebAIM's Contrast Checker to test any palette.
Typography Basics

Typography is the art of arranging text so it is readable, beautiful, and appropriate for the message.

  • Serif vs. Sans-Serif: Serif fonts (Georgia, Times New Roman) have decorative "feet" — they feel traditional and trustworthy. Sans-serif fonts (Roboto, Inter, Arial) feel modern and clean on screens. Most websites use sans-serif for body text.
  • Font Hierarchy: Size and weight signal importance — H1 is the largest and boldest, H2 is slightly smaller, body text is comfortable for reading, and captions are the smallest. Never skip heading levels.
  • Line Height (Leading): The vertical space between lines of text. Too tight = hard to read. Too loose = feels disconnected. The sweet spot for body text is 1.4–1.6× the font size.
  • Kerning & Tracking: Kerning adjusts the space between specific character pairs (e.g., "AV"). Tracking adjusts spacing uniformly across a word or block of text — used to make all-caps headers more readable.
  • Web Fonts: Google Fonts and Adobe Fonts deliver professional typefaces via CDN. Use a <link> tag to load them — no downloads required. Common picks: Roboto, Open Sans, Lato, Playfair Display.
  • The 2-Font Rule: Use a maximum of 2–3 fonts per project. A typical pairing: one serif for headings, one sans-serif for body text. Mixing more creates visual chaos.
How Users Read: F-Pattern & Z-Pattern

Eye-tracking research proves users don't read websites — they scan. Design for scanning, not reading.

  • F-Pattern: On content-heavy pages (articles, search results, product listings), the eye scans horizontally across the top, then back to the left, then a shorter horizontal scan, then straight down the left margin — forming an "F." Put your key information along the top and left edge.
  • Z-Pattern: On simple pages (landing pages, ads, homepages), the eye starts top-left → top-right → diagonal → bottom-left → bottom-right — forming a "Z." Place your logo top-left, navigation top-right, and your call-to-action (CTA) button at the bottom-right of the Z.

The Blueprint: How Websites Are Made

Professional websites are not built in one sitting. Every successful web project — from a local restaurant menu site to an enterprise e-commerce platform — follows a Web Development Life Cycle (WDLC). This is a repeating, structured process that ensures the final product actually solves the client's problem. Skipping phases is how projects go over budget, miss deadlines, and disappoint clients.

The 8-Phase Web Development Life Cycle
  1. Research & Analysis — Understand the client, their competitors, and their target audience before a single line of code is written. Who are the users? What problem does the site solve? What do competitors do well or poorly? This phase produces a creative brief and competitive analysis.
  2. Planning — Define scope, timeline, and budget. Create a sitemap (the page structure) and identify all required functionality. A signed project agreement protects both agency and client. This is where the Project Management Triangle (Fast / Good / Cheap — pick two) becomes real.
  3. Design — Translate research into visual concepts. Wireframes sketch layout without color. Mockups in Figma or Adobe XD add color palette, typography, and imagery. Client approves the mockup before development begins.
  4. Content — Gather or create all words, photos, videos, and files that populate the site. Content is the biggest bottleneck in most projects — clients routinely underestimate how much effort this requires. Without real content, development is just building a shell.
  5. Development — Write the actual code: HTML for structure, CSS for visual style, JavaScript for interactivity. Connect to databases or APIs if needed. Build the CMS if the client will update content themselves. This is the primary skill this course trains you for.
  6. Testing — Systematically break the site before users do. Test across browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge) and screen sizes. Check for broken links, slow load times, accessibility issues (WCAG), form validation, and security vulnerabilities. Document and fix every bug found.
  7. Deployment — Move the site from development to the live production server. Configure DNS records, set up SSL/HTTPS, run final checks. Modern agencies use CI/CD pipelines to automate safe, fast deployments.
  8. Maintenance — The site is live — the work continues. Update content, patch security vulnerabilities, monitor uptime, optimize performance, and respond to client feedback. Most agencies offer monthly retainer packages, creating recurring revenue. Then the cycle begins again with new research.
Agency Insight: Why the Cycle Never Ends

Notice that after Maintenance, the cycle restarts with Research. User behavior changes, technology evolves, search engine algorithms update, and client businesses grow. A website that is not actively maintained will decay — security holes appear, speed degrades, content becomes outdated, and competitors overtake it in search rankings. The most successful agencies sell ongoing maintenance contracts, not one-time builds. Recurring revenue is the goal.

The Art of Not Having a Terrible Meeting

Quick question: have you ever been in a group where everyone is talking over each other, no decisions get made, and 45 minutes later everyone leaves more frustrated than when they started? That's what happens without a system. Parliamentary Procedure is that system — and every professional organization on earth uses some version of it.

What Is Parliamentary Procedure?

Parliamentary Procedure is a standardized set of rules for conducting formal meetings fairly and efficiently. Think of it as the official rulebook for group decision-making. It makes sure that everyone gets a fair chance to speak, decisions are made by the group and not hijacked by whoever yells loudest, and meetings actually end with something decided.

The most widely used version is Robert's Rules of Order, written in 1876 by U.S. Army General Henry Martyn Robert after he got frustrated running a chaotic church meeting and decided to write down a better system. It caught on everywhere — from Congress to your local school board to Fortune 500 companies to SkillsUSA chapter meetings.

As a future agency leader, you'll use these same rules to run project kickoff meetings, design reviews, and any time your team needs to make a real decision together.

The 8-Step Motion Combo: How Decisions Actually Happen

In a formal meeting, every decision starts as a motion — a formal proposal. Here's the exact sequence (think of it like a combo move):

  1. Member addresses the Chair: "Mr./Madam President..."
  2. Chair recognizes the member — gives them the floor
  3. Member states the motion: "I move that we allocate $200 for competition registration fees."
  4. Another member seconds it: "Second!" (This doesn't mean they agree — it just means enough people want to discuss it)
  5. Chair restates the motion and opens discussion
  6. Open discussion: Members debate pros and cons — each person speaks once before anyone speaks a second time
  7. Vote: "All in favor say 'Aye.' All opposed say 'Nay.'"
  8. Chair announces the result: "The motion carries" or "The motion fails."

The Professional Workflow: Agile & Feedback

In the professional world, we use the Agile methodology: a flexible project management system used to build software in small, iterative steps. We work in Sprints (usually 2 weeks) to finish specific tasks. This is exactly how your agency will manage building sites for clients.

Giving Feedback (The "Crit-Wich")

We use the Critique Sandwich to deliver feedback without causing conflict: start with a positive, give the "meat" (the criticism), and end with another positive.

Receiving Feedback (Don't Shield-Bash!)

Don't get defensive! Use Empathy: the ability to understand and share the feelings and frustrations of the user to build a better product. When a team lead suggests a change, remember that feedback is a gift that saves you from a failed launch.

The "Project Triangle" (A Law of the Universe)

Every project is a balance of three things: Time (fast), Cost (cheap), and Quality (good). The rule is: You can only pick TWO.

  • Fast + Good = Expensive.
  • Fast + Cheap = Low-Quality (buggy).
  • Good + Cheap = Takes a long time.

Professional teams often start with a Minimum Viable Product (MVP)—a version of a product with just enough features to be usable by early customers who can then provide feedback.

Rule #1: File Naming Conventions
  1. ALWAYS name your homepage index.html. (All lowercase). Servers look for this "front door" automatically.
  2. File names must be all-lowercase. Linux servers are case-sensitive.
  3. NEVER use spaces in file or folder names. Use a hyphen (-).

Your Digital "Code of Conduct" (Legal & Ethics)
Intellectual Property (IP)

Everything created with the mind is Intellectual Property. There are three main shields:

  • Copyright ©: Automatic protection for creative works (code, photos, text).
  • Trademark ™: Protects brand identity (logos like Nike's swoosh).
  • Patent: Protects new inventions for ~20 years. You must apply for this; it's not automatic.

To share work legally, we use Creative Commons licenses or use work from the Public Domain (where copyright has expired).

Ethics, Access & Accessibility

Avoid "Dark Patterns"—deceptive UI tricks meant to manipulate users. Professional designers use Responsive Design (layouts that adapt to any screen) and Accessibility (ensuring the site works for everyone, including those with disabilities). In the famous Domino's Lawsuit, the Supreme Court ruled that websites are public accommodations and must be accessible. This is often called the "Curb Cut Effect"—when you design for marginalized users, you make things better for everyone.

Security: Hashing & Salting

Safety is a Guild rule. Never store passwords as plain text. Use hashing and salting to create irreversible, un-readable fingerprints of data.
