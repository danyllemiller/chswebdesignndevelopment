Whose Stuff Is This Anyway? A Guide to Intellectual Property

This is the absolute number one rule of the internet: Don't steal stuff. It's the digital golden rule. Just because you can right-click-save a picture, copy a block of code, or download a song doesn't mean it's yours to use. Everything someone creates with their mind is their Intellectual Property (IP), and it's protected by powerful laws. Let's break down the "Big Three" force fields that guard those creations.

1. Copyright © (The Automatic Shield)

Copyright is an automatic shield that protects creative works. The second you write a poem, snap a photo, record a video, or code a cool JavaScript game, it's copyrighted. You own it. You don't have to file any paperwork, register with a fancy office, or even type the © symbol. It's yours. This shield protects books, music, photos, videos, articles, and yes, even software code. You can research the law further at the U.S. Copyright Office.

Work for Hire: If you are a professional developer, your contract will likely state that the company owns the copyright to anything you build on the clock. This is called "Work Made for Hire."

2. Trademark ™ (The Brand Sentry)

This doesn't protect a creative work; it protects a brand. A trademark is a specific word, phrase, logo, or even a sound that represents a company. Think of the Nike "swoosh," the name "Coca-Cola," or the golden arches of McDonald's. These are all fiercely protected. You can't just slap a famous logo on your website to sell t-shirts. That's called infringement, and it's a great way to get a very scary, very real letter from a team of expensive lawyers. The law exists to prevent any confusion in the marketplace. Check the USPTO database to see what brands are protected.

3. Patent ℗ (The Invention Ledger)

A patent protects a new, specific, and non-obvious way of doing something. This could be a new type of smartphone technology, a medical device, or even a piece of software technology, like Amazon's original 1-Click purchase system. As a web designer, you'll bump into this less often than copyright or trademark, but it's a huge part of the tech world that protects the "how-it-works" part of a new idea. Research the Public Domain rules to see when patents expire. Patents are the only IP type that is not automatic; you must apply for them and they expire after about 20 years.

The Sharing Economy: Creative Commons and the Public Domain

Does all this mean you have to create every single pixel on your site from scratch? Thankfully, no. Many creators want to share their work. That's where Creative Commons (CC) comes in. Think of it as a "How You Can Share My Food" menu that creators can attach to their work.

Instead of the default "All Rights Reserved" of copyright, CC provides a range of "Some Rights Reserved." A creator might say you can use their photo for free, but their license will have rules:

  • Attribution (BY): "You can have my pizza, but you must tell everyone I made it." This is the core requirement of almost all CC licenses.
  • NonCommercial (NC): "You can have my pizza, but you can't sell slices of it." You cannot make money using these assets. (Note: Your agency cannot use NC assets for paying clients!)
  • NoDerivatives (ND): "You can have my pizza, but you can't add pineapples." You cannot edit, crop, or filter the work.
  • ShareAlike (SA): If you remix the work, you must share your final product under the exact same license.

These licenses often get stacked. You'll frequently see "BY-NC," which means "Give me credit and don't use it to make money." Before you use any CC-licensed work, you must read the license and follow its rules to the letter at CreativeCommons.org.

Public Domain & The "Fair Use" Trap

The Public Domain is like a "free buffet." When a copyright expires or if a creator voluntarily gives up all their rights, their work belongs to everyone. You can use it for anything with no credit needed. Check Project Gutenberg for examples of public domain literature.

The "Don't Touch" Rule: If you cannot find a license, you must assume you CANNOT use it. The default setting of the entire internet is "All Rights Reserved." "I found it on Google Images" is not a legal defense. It is an admission that you took something without checking if you had permission.

What about Fair Use? Fair Use is a limited legal defense, not a right. It considers four factors: purpose (educational vs. commercial), nature of the work, amount used, and effect on the market. In the professional guild, we avoid the Fair Use gray area and stick to licensed or original content.

Build a Fortress: Web Security and User Privacy

A great website isn't just legal—it's safe. When users visit your site, they are trusting you with their time, their attention, and often their data. You have a legal and ethical responsibility to protect them. Building a website is like building a house; if you don't build a strong foundation and lock the doors, don't be surprised when people get hurt.

Your Digital Ghost: Footprints & Privacy

Every single thing you do online—your searches, your "likes," your comments, every site you visit, even how long you pause to look at a picture—is tracked and recorded. This massive trail of data is your Digital Footprint. Think of it like an internet tattoo. It's permanent, it's searchable, and people will judge you for it. Explore your own footprint with tools like the EFF's Privacy Badger.

Now, imagine your school principal reading all your texts. That's a gross, creepy feeling, right? That feeling is an Invasion of Privacy. It's when personal information (PII - Personally Identifiable Information) is collected or shared without permission. As a developer, if you build a site that collects user emails and then you sell that email list to advertisers, you are the one invading their privacy. That's not just unethical; it's often illegal under modern data laws.

The Secret Handshake: HTTP vs. HTTPS

Have you ever noticed that some website addresses start with http:// and others start with https://? That one little 's' is arguably the most important letter in all of web security.

HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol) is like sending a postcard. Anyone who intercepts it—a hacker on the same coffee shop Wi-Fi—can read everything on it in plain text. HTTPS (Secure) is like sending a sealed, armored letter. The information is encrypted (scrambled into an unbreakable secret code). You get the 's' and the padlock icon 🔒 by installing a Security Certificate (SSL/TLS). It proves your site is who it says it is via a trusted Certificate Authority (CA).

The SSL/TLS Handshake (The 8-Step Secret)
  1. Request: Browser asks for a secure session.
  2. Certificate: Server sends its SSL Certificate and Public Key.
  3. Verification: Browser checks the certificate with the CA.
  4. Session Key Creation: Browser creates a unique, temporary Session Key.
  5. Asymmetric Encryption: Browser encrypts that Session Key using the server's Public Key.
  6. Private Unlock: Server uses its Private Key to unlock the message.
  7. Mutual Knowledge: Now both share a secret "code" that no one else knows.
  8. Symmetric Encryption: All data is now scrambled using that secret key.
The Security Checklist
  • ALWAYS use HTTPS. Period. Browsers will now punish your SEO and warn users with a "Not Secure" red banner if you don't. Test your site with the Qualys SSL Test.
  • Clear Privacy Policy. You must have a page on your site that tells users what data you are collecting in plain English. See an example at our CCSD notices page.
  • Data Protection. Don't store passwords in plain text; use hashing and salting (one-way-scrambling) to protect your database.

Privacy Laws: GDPR vs. Nevada

Governments have stepped in to protect users. First is the GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) from Europe. It uses an "OPT-IN" model. You cannot collect data unless the user gives specific consent. It also grants the "Right to be Forgotten," where users can demand their data be deleted. Failure to comply can cost a company 4% of their global revenue.

Here in our state, we have the Nevada Consumer Privacy Act (SB220). It uses an "OPT-OUT" model focused on the Sale of Data. It gives residents the right to tell a website, "STOP SELLING MY DATA" via a clear link. This represents the American philosophy of "Business First, with Consumer Protection," whereas the European model is "Privacy First."

As a developer, you must build for the strictest laws to ensure your site is legal globally. This is known as Privacy by Design.

Make the Web for Everyone: Accessibility (a11y)

The ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) makes accessibility a legal requirement for "places of public accommodation," which courts now say include websites. Professional developers follow the WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines), the global standard for inclusive design. WCAG is divided into three compliance levels: Level A (Minimum), Level AA (Standard/Legal Target), and Level AAA (Maximum).

The POUR Principles: The 4 Pillars of a11y

To be accessible, a website must meet these four criteria established by the W3C WAI:

  • Perceivable: Users must be able to perceive the information. If it's visual, there must be a text alternative (Alt Text). If it's audio, there must be captions. It shouldn't depend on just one sense.
  • Operable: The interface cannot require interactions that a user cannot perform. A site that requires a mouse is not operable for a user with tremors or blindness. Everything must be reachable via keyboard.
  • Understandable: Both the information and the operation of the user interface must be understandable. Don't use confusing navigation or jargon that prevents a user from knowing "what happens next."
  • Robust: Content must be robust enough to be interpreted by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies like screen readers (NVDA, JAWS, or VoiceOver).
The Guild Audit: Checking for the "Big 3"
  • Alt Text for Images: Screen readers read code aloud for blind users. If your logo's alt text is empty or says "logo.png," a blind user has no idea which site they are on. Rule: Use descriptive text for images (e.g., alt="Carson High School Logo") and use alt="" for purely decorative images so screen readers skip them.
  • Color Contrast: Use a 4.5:1 ratio for normal text and 3:1 for large text. If you use light gray text on a white background, it's invisible to users with visual impairments. Use the WebAIM Contrast Checker to verify your palette.
  • Keyboard Navigation: Users who can't use a mouse must be able to "tab" through your site. If they can't reach the "Buy" button with a keyboard, the site is broken. Always ensure there is a visible Focus Ring so users know where they are.
The Technical Edge: ARIA and Semantic HTML

The easiest way to be accessible is to use Semantic HTML (e.g., using <button> instead of <div onclick="...">). When standard HTML isn't enough, we use ARIA (Accessible Rich Internet Applications) roles and attributes to tell assistive technology exactly what a custom component does.

Case Study: The Domino's Lawsuit. Domino's Pizza was sued because a blind user couldn't order a pizza on their site using a screen reader. The case went all the way to the Supreme Court, and Domino's lost. This proved that accessibility is not a choice; it is a civil right.

Your Digital Compass: Ethics and NDAs

Laws define the floor—the minimum you must do. Ethics define the ceiling—what you should do. Model legal and ethical use of information: Cite your sources, respect privacy, and avoid Dark Patterns. Dark patterns are UI designs intended to trick users into choices they didn't mean to make, like "Sneak into Basket" fees or "Forced Continuity" subscriptions.

The Secret-Keeper's Oath: Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDA)

In the professional tech world, a Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) is more than just a "pinky promise" 🤫. It is a legally binding contract that creates a "confidential relationship" between you and an employer or client. It is the primary tool used to protect Trade Secrets—proprietary information that gives a business a competitive edge.

Anatomy of a Professional NDA

When you sign your first contract for an internship or job, expect these standard clauses:

  • The Scope of Confidentiality: This section defines exactly what is secret. It often includes source code, internal logic, UI mockups, client lists, and unreleased project codenames.
  • Unilateral vs. Mutual: A Unilateral NDA is one-way (you keep their secrets). A Mutual NDA is two-way (you both keep each other's secrets, common in startup partnerships).
  • The Term (Survival Clause): Secrets don't always expire. Most NDAs last 2 to 5 years, but Trade Secrets (like the Google Search algorithm) are protected indefinitely. Even after you leave a job, your obligation to keep their secrets "survives" the end of your employment.
  • Remedies for Breach: If you leak information, the company can seek Injunctive Relief (a court order to stop you immediately) and Liquidated Damages (pre-set massive fines).
The "Blackball" and Real-World Scenarios

If you break an NDA—for example, by leaking a screenshot of a new game level to Discord—you aren't just fired. You can be sued for massive sums, and you will likely be blackballed from the industry. Tech is a small world; your reputation for keeping secrets is your most valuable asset.

  • Scenario A: The "GTA VI" Leak. Leaking footage or code of an unreleased high-profile game can result in criminal charges and civil lawsuits for millions in lost marketing value.
  • Scenario B: The Freelancer's Logic. You cannot take a secret encryption algorithm from a fintech client and sell it to their competitor. This is "Theft of Trade Secrets."
  • Scenario C: The Interview Leak. Many companies (like Google or Apple) require you to sign an NDA just to interview so you don't leak their internal test questions or future plans.

Check Your Bias at the Door

We all have biases—shortcuts our brains make based on our own limited experiences. The problem is that these biases can sneak into our code and end up hurting real people. When these human shortcuts enter code, it becomes Algorithmic Bias. Computers are reflective: they learn from the data we give them. Bias usually enters through the Training Dataset. For example, if you build a hiring AI and train it on resumes from the last 10 years (mostly men), the AI will "learn" that men are better and dock points for resumes containing the word "women's."

Real-World Case Studies:
  • The Racist Soap Dispenser: Infrared sensors that only activated for light skin tones because they weren't tested on darker skin. Darkness absorbs infrared light; the sensors were "too cheap" to detect it.
  • The Biased Hiring AI: An algorithm that learned "men are better" because it was trained on 10 years of male-dominated resumes.
  • Healthcare Inequity: Algorithms that prioritized white patients over Black patients because they used "previous healthcare cost" as a shortcut for "medical need," ignoring that Black patients had historically been denied access to expensive care.
Inclusive Design: The "Curb Cut" Effect

Inclusive design is building for the most marginalized users to make the experience better for everyone. This is called the Curb Cut Effect. Sidewalk ramps (curb cuts) were designed for wheelchairs, but they also benefit parents with strollers and travelers with suitcases. In web design, high contrast helps the vision-impaired, but it also helps the user trying to read their phone in direct sunlight. Explore the work of the Algorithmic Justice League (AJL) to learn more.

Inclusive Checklist:

  • Are gender selections inclusive?
  • Do my stock photos represent a diverse range of skin tones and abilities?
  • Is my text written at a readable level for all users?
