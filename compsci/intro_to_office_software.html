0.1 Computer Basics & Troubleshooting

Before you can write code or create presentations, you have to successfully operate the physical machine. In a professional or lab environment, hardware configurations can be tricky.

0.1.1 Powering On & Hardware Components

In a desktop computer setup, the computer and the screen are two completely separate pieces of hardware.

  • The PC Tower: This is the actual "brain" of the computer (containing the CPU, RAM, and storage). It has its own power cable connected to the wall and a main power button (usually on the front or top).
  • The Monitor: This is just a display screen. It has its own power cable and power button. If the PC is on but the monitor is off, your screen will remain black.

0.1.2 Input vs. Output Devices

  • Input Devices: Send data INTO the computer (Keyboard, Mouse, Microphone, Webcam).
  • Output Devices: Receive data OUT OF the computer (Monitor, Speakers, Printer).

0.1.3 Troubleshooting: "It won't turn on!"

If you press the power button and nothing happens, do not panic. Follow this basic hardware trace:

  1. Check the Power Strip: Is it plugged into the wall? Is the switch flipped to the "ON" or "RESET" position (usually indicated by a glowing light)?
  2. Check the Power Cables: Ensure the thick black power cables are pushed firmly into the back of the PC tower, the monitor, and the power strip.
  3. Check the Display Cable: Ensure the display cable (HDMI or DisplayPort) is securely plugged into both the back of the monitor and the back of the PC tower.

0.2 File Management Basics

A computer without organization quickly becomes a digital junkyard. You should always create specific Folders (e.g., Documents/Computer_Science/Unit_0/) to group related files rather than dumping everything onto your Desktop.

Local Storage vs. Cloud Storage
  • Local Storage (C: Drive / Desktop): The file lives physically inside the hardware of the specific computer you are using. If the hard drive crashes, you lose your work.
  • Cloud Storage (Google Drive / OneDrive): The file lives on the internet. It automatically saves every few seconds, and you can access it from any device by logging into your account.

0.3 Word Processing Mastery

Typing words onto a screen is easy; structuring information so that it is readable, professional, and accessible requires mastery. Word processors like Microsoft Word and Google Docs are powerful tools if you know how to use them beyond just basic typing.

0.3.1 The Anatomy of a Document

Before you start typing, you need to understand the workspace. Modern word processors use a standardized layout:

  • The Ribbon/Toolbar: The menu at the top containing all your tools, organized into tabs (Home, Insert, Layout).
  • The Ruler: Located at the top and left edges, this tool allows you to visually set indentations, tabs, and page margins.
  • Margins: The blank white space around the edge of the page. Standard academic and professional margins are usually 1 inch on all sides.

0.3.2 Formatting for Readability

Have you ever looked at a massive "wall of text" and immediately lost the motivation to read it? Proper formatting prevents this by using Hierarchy.

Information Hierarchy (H1, H2, P)
  • Heading 1 (H1): The main title of the document. There should usually only be one H1.
  • Heading 2 (H2): Major section breaks (like "0.1 Word Processing Mastery" above).
  • Paragraph (P) / Normal Text: The actual body of your content. Using proper line spacing (like 1.15 or 1.5) and alignment (Left-aligned for easiest reading) makes a massive difference in professional communication.

0.3.3 Creating, Formatting & Sharing (Google Docs vs. MS Word)

Creating & Formatting
  • Create: In Google Drive, click "+ New" > Google Docs. In Word, open the app and click "Blank Document".
  • Add a Title/Heading: Type your text, highlight it, and click the "Normal text" dropdown in the toolbar. Select "Title" or "Heading 1".
  • Change Font & Color: Highlight text. Click the Font Name dropdown (e.g., Arial) to change styles. Click the A with a line under it to change text color.
  • Add Images: Click Insert > Image. Once inserted, click the image and drag the blue corner squares to resize it safely.
Saving & Sharing
  • Saving (Docs): Auto-saves to the cloud. Click "Untitled document" in the top-left to name it.
  • Saving (Word): Click File > Save As, choose a folder, and name it. Press Ctrl+S frequently!
  • Sharing (Docs): Click the blue "Share" button. Change General Access to "Anyone with the link" and copy/paste that link into an email or assignment submission.
  • Sharing (Word): Save the .docx file locally, open your email, click the Paperclip (Attach) icon, and locate the file.

0.3.4 The Power of the PDF

If you email a carefully formatted Microsoft Word document to someone using an old Mac or a different app, all your margins, fonts, and spacing might break. How do we prevent this?

The Working File (.docx)

A .docx or Google Doc is "fluid." It is meant to be edited. Anyone who opens it can accidentally delete a paragraph or mess up the formatting.

The Locked File (.pdf)

A Portable Document Format (PDF) takes a "digital snapshot" of your document. It locks the fonts, images, and margins in place so it looks exactly the same on every device, operating system, and printer in the world.

Step-by-Step: How to Export as a PDF

To ensure your document formatting never breaks, always submit final assignments as a PDF. Here is how to create one in the two most popular word processors:

From Google Docs/Slides:
  1. Click File in the top-left menu bar.
  2. Hover your mouse over Download in the dropdown menu.
  3. Click PDF Document (.pdf). The locked file will instantly download to your computer.
From Microsoft Word/PowerPoint:
  1. Click File in the top-left menu bar.
  2. Select Save As (or Export, depending on your version).
  3. Click the "File Format" dropdown menu (which usually says ".docx") and change it to PDF.
  4. Click Save.

0.4 Powerful Presentations

Presentations are not documents. A document is meant to be read; a presentation is a visual aid meant to support a speaker. Mixing up the two results in bored, disengaged audiences.

0.4.1 Death by PowerPoint

The worst mistake you can make is creating "Death by PowerPoint." Avoid these common pitfalls:

  • Too Much Text: If your audience is reading paragraphs off the screen, they are not listening to you.
  • Reading from the Screen: Never turn your back to the audience to read your own slides verbatim.
  • Terrible Contrast: Using light yellow text on a white background makes your presentation unreadable.

0.4.2 Principles of Slide Design

To create a powerful slide deck, embrace these core design principles:

  • White Space: Empty space is your friend. It draws the eye to what actually matters.
  • High-Quality Imagery: Use one strong, high-resolution photo instead of five tiny, pixelated clip-art images.
  • Consistent Branding: Pick a color palette (2-3 colors) and 1 or 2 fonts, and stick to them for the entire presentation.

0.4.3 Choosing the Right Tool

Different tasks require different software. Standard tools like PowerPoint, Keynote, or Google Slides are great for standard classroom or business presentations. However, if you are designing a highly visual "Pitch Deck" for a startup, you might want to use creative layout tools like Canva, Adobe Express, or Adobe InDesign.

0.4.4 Creating & Designing (Google Slides vs. PowerPoint)

Working with Text & Themes
  • Applying a Theme: Click Slide > Change Theme (or the Theme button on the toolbar) to instantly apply professional background colors and matching fonts to all slides.
  • Adding Text Boxes: Unlike Word, you cannot just click anywhere and type. Text must live inside a Text Box. Click the 'T' icon in the toolbar, draw a box, and type inside it.
  • Adding Images: Click Insert > Image. You can drag images anywhere on the slide.
Saving & Sharing
  • Saving: Google Slides auto-saves. For MS PowerPoint, click File > Save As.
  • Sharing Warning: Presentations with lots of images create massive file sizes. If your .pptx file is over 25MB, you cannot attach it to a standard email. You must upload it to a cloud drive and share the link, or share directly via Google Slides.

0.5 Spreadsheet Fundamentals

Google Sheets and MS Excel are used for organizing data, tracking budgets, and performing math calculations. Data is organized into a grid of Columns (letters) and Rows (numbers).

0.5.1 Cells & Data Entry

The intersection of a Column and a Row is called a Cell (e.g., cell A1 is the top-left box). To add data, click a cell so it is highlighted, type your text/numbers, and press Enter to move down, or Tab to move right.

0.5.2 Equations & Formulas

Spreadsheets do math for you. To tell the software you are writing a math equation (and not just typing regular text), you must always start by typing an Equals Sign ( = ).

The Top 5 Essential Formulas:
  • =SUM(A1:A5) Adds all numbers from cell A1 through A5 together.
  • =AVERAGE(B1:B10) Calculates the average (mean) of the selected cells.
  • =MAX(C1:C20) Finds the highest number in the selected range.
  • =MIN(D1:D20) Finds the lowest number in the selected range.
  • =COUNT(E1:E10) Counts how many cells actually have numbers in them.

0.6 Collaboration & Teamwork Standards 9-12.AP.PD.4, 9-12.IC.SI.1

In the modern workplace, you will rarely work alone. The ability to seamlessly collaborate on digital files with team members across the room (or across the globe) is a mandatory skill.

Concurrent Editing

We used to email files back and forth: project_v1.docx, then project_v2_final.docx, then project_v3_really_final.docx. It was a mess.

Today, we use Cloud Collaboration (like Google Docs or Office 365). Multiple people can log into the exact same document at the exact same time (concurrent editing). You can see their cursors typing live, eliminating the need to ever email a file attachment.

Version History & Tracking Changes

What happens if your partner accidentally deletes half your essay? Because you are in the cloud, you can open Version History to literally turn back time and restore the document to how it looked 3 hours ago.

Instead of deleting someone's work, you should use Suggested Edits and Comment Threads. This allows you to communicate asynchronously and approve changes before they become permanent.

Digital Etiquette & Permissions

When sharing a file, you must assign the correct permission level to protect your data:

  • Viewer: Can only read the file. (Best for public sharing).
  • Commenter: Can read and leave sticky notes, but cannot change the actual text.
  • Editor: Has full control to delete and rewrite. (Only give this to trusted teammates).

0.7 Digital Literacy & Keyboard Shortcuts

Power users don't take their hands off the keyboard to hunt for options in menus. Memorizing these essential keyboard shortcuts will drastically increase your speed and efficiency.

  • Universal Text Editing
  • Copy Ctrl + C
  • Cut (removes original) Ctrl + X
  • Paste Ctrl + V
  • Select All Ctrl + A
  • Undo (The Lifesaver) Ctrl + Z
  • System & Browser
  • Save File Ctrl + S
  • Find (Search inside a document) Ctrl + F
  • Switch Programs/Windows Alt + Tab
  • New Browser Tab Ctrl + T
  • Restore Closed Tab Ctrl + Shift + T

0.8 Basic Email & Netiquette

Email is the official communication tool of the professional world. Sending an email to a teacher or boss is very different from sending a text message.

0.8.1 Anatomy of a Professional Email

  • The Subject Line: A short, clear summary (e.g., "Question about Chapter 3"). Never leave this blank.
  • The Salutation: Always open professionally (e.g., "Dear Mr. Miller,").
  • The Body: Keep your message clear, polite, and to the point.
  • The Sign-off: Close professionally (e.g., "Thank you, [Your Name]").

0.8.2 Netiquette (Internet Etiquette)

What NOT to do:
  • Do not type in ALL CAPS (it reads as shouting).
  • Do not use text-speak or excessive emojis ("idk lol").
  • Do not demand immediate responses.
What TO do:
  • Always proofread for spelling before sending.
  • Keep your tone respectful and neutral.
  • Include context. Say "I need help with Step 4" rather than just "I need help."
