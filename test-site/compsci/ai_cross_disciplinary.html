18.1 Algorithms Across Disciplines Standard 9-12.IC.C.3

An algorithm doesn't care what kind of data it is processing. The exact same mathematical logic used to sort a high-score leaderboard in a video game can be used to solve completely different problems across multiple industries.

18.1.1 The "Universal Tool"

Consider a "Pathfinding Algorithm." In a video game, this algorithm calculates the shortest path for an enemy character to walk around walls and attack the player. That is the exact same algorithmic logic used by:

  • Logistics (FedEx/UPS): Calculating the fastest driving route to deliver 150 packages while avoiding traffic jams and left turns.
  • Biology: Analyzing the sequence of proteins in DNA to find the shortest path to a genetic mutation.
  • Music Streaming: Mapping the relationships between millions of songs to find the perfect "path" of recommendations to keep a listener engaged.

18.1.2 Computing in the Sciences

Science is no longer just done in physical laboratories with test tubes and microscopes. It is done inside the CPU. Scientists use the massive Computational Models we learned about in Unit 4 to simulate chemical reactions that would take thousands of years to occur naturally, or to track complex wildlife migration patterns using thousands of GPS data points plotted in a database.

18.1.3 Digital Arts & Humanities

The humanities and arts are deeply intertwined with computer science. Digital Arts rely entirely on code: CGI (Computer-Generated Imagery) in modern film-making requires rendering farms calculating the real-world physics of light bouncing off a 3D model. Digital music production uses complex mathematical waves to synthesize entirely new instruments.

Even historians use computing! By scanning thousands of ancient, decaying manuscripts into a database, historians use Natural Language Processing (NLP) algorithms to instantly find hidden historical patterns or translate dead languages.

18.2 The Rise of Artificial Intelligence Standard 9-12.IC.C.4

For decades, computers only did exactly what humans explicitly programmed them to do. Today, we are teaching computers how to learn and make decisions on their own. This is the era of Artificial Intelligence.

18.2.1 What is AI, Really?

When people hear "AI," they often think of scary sci-fi robots. In reality, Artificial Intelligence is simply the science of making machines mimic human cognitive functions, like learning, recognizing patterns, and solving problems. An AI doesn't have a soul or a conscience; it has an unimaginably complex set of mathematical probabilities.

18.2.2 Machine Learning (The "Pattern Finder")

Instead of a human writing a rule that says IF photo has two pointy ears AND a tail, THEN it is a cat, we use Machine Learning (ML). We feed a computer 10 million photos of cats (the "Big Data" we studied in Unit 3). The computer mathematically analyzes the pixels and figures out its own rules for what makes a cat. The more data we feed it, the smarter and more accurate its pattern recognition becomes.

18.2.3 Generative AI

If Machine Learning is great at "identifying" things, Generative AI (like ChatGPT, Midjourney, or DALL-E) is great at "creating" things. By analyzing billions of human conversations, books, and images, Generative AI learns the mathematical probability of what word or pixel should come next.

When you ask an AI to write a poem, it doesn't "think" about poetry. It calculates: "Based on 10 million poems I've analyzed, if the first word is 'Roses', there is a 98% mathematical probability the next word should be 'are', followed by 'red'."

Step-by-Step: The Art of Prompt Engineering

Because Generative AI is just a probability engine, a vague request will generate a vague response. To get professional results, you must master "Prompt Engineering" using the RTCF Framework (Role, Task, Context, Format).

  1. Role: Tell the AI who it should act like.
    "Act as an expert high school computer science teacher."
  2. Task: Give a highly specific instruction.
    "Explain how a 'For Loop' works."
  3. Context: Provide the background information it needs to narrow its focus.
    "The explanation should be easy to understand for a student who has never written code before and likes video games."
  4. Format: Tell the AI exactly how you want the output structured.
    "Provide the answer as a short, 3-bullet-point list with a Minecraft analogy at the end."

18.3 The Impact of AI on Society Standard 9-12.IC.C.4

AI is the most powerful tool humanity has invented since electricity. Like electricity, it has the power to illuminate the world, but it also has the power to burn the house down if we do not use it ethically.

Curing and Protecting

AI is already saving lives. In healthcare, Machine Learning algorithms can analyze an MRI scan and detect early signs of cancer months before a human doctor could see them. In climate science, AI is optimizing our power grids to drastically reduce electricity waste, and in education, AI tutors can adapt in real-time to teach a student at their exact learning pace.

Bias, Fakes, and the "Black Box"

The risks are equally massive. Generative AI allows for the creation of "Deepfakes"—highly realistic, fake videos of people doing or saying things they never did, which can destroy reputations or swing elections.

There is also the "Black Box" Problem. Because Machine Learning AIs write their own rules based on billions of data points, sometimes humans don't actually know why an AI made a decision. If an AI denies someone a loan, and we can't look at the code to see why, how do we know the AI isn't exhibiting algorithmic bias?

Changing Tasks, Not Just Replacing Humans

Will AI take your job? History shows that technology rarely destroys all jobs; instead, it shifts them. When the tractor was invented, farmers didn't disappear—they learned how to drive tractors.

AI will likely automate the repetitive tasks of your job (like organizing data, writing basic emails, or summarizing reports), freeing you up to focus on the deeply human skills that AI cannot replicate: empathy, creative problem solving, and complex ethical leadership.
