4.1 The Blueprint of a Network Standard 9-12.NI.NCO.1

In Unit 1, we learned how a single computer processes data. But a computer operating in isolation is like a brain with no mouth or ears. The true power of modern computing comes from networks—allowing millions of machines to share data, resources, and processing power instantly.

4.1.1 Nodes and Links

Every network, whether it connects two laptops in a bedroom or billions of servers across the globe, is built on two fundamental concepts:

  • Nodes: A node is any physical device connected to the network. This includes computers, printers, smartphones, smart TVs, and the routers that direct the traffic.
  • Links: A link is the connection path between two nodes. Links can be physical (like copper Ethernet cables or glass Fiber Optic cables) or wireless (like Wi-Fi radio waves or 5G cell signals).

4.1.2 LAN vs. WAN

Networks are categorized by their geographic scale. The two most common types you interact with daily are LANs and WANs.

LAN (Local Area Network)

A LAN connects nodes within a small, specific geographic area. Your home Wi-Fi network, the computers in a single classroom, or the devices in a small office building all form a LAN.

WAN (Wide Area Network)

A WAN spans a large geographic distance, connecting multiple LANs together. The Internet itself is the ultimate WAN—a massive, global network of local networks communicating across oceans via undersea cables.

4.1.3 The Hardware Hierarchy (The "Middlemen")

When you send an email, it doesn't magically jump from your laptop directly to your friend's phone. It travels through specialized hardware nodes designed to direct traffic.

  • Hubs vs. Switches (Local Directors): Both devices connect multiple computers within a LAN. A Hub is "dumb"—if one computer sends a message to a printer, the Hub shouts that message to every single device plugged into it, creating network traffic jams. A Switch is "smart"—it learns which device is plugged into which port and sends the message only to the intended target.
  • Routers (The Post Office): A router's job is to connect different networks together. Your home router connects your private LAN to your Internet Service Provider's massive WAN. It reads the "address" on the data and determines the fastest route to get it there.
  • Servers (The Librarians): Powerful, high-capacity computers designed to store files, host websites, and "serve" data to client devices whenever requested.

4.2 Network Topology & Addressing Standard 9-12.NI.NCO.1

How do we physically arrange the cables connecting our computers? And once they are connected, how does one computer know how to "call" another? This requires structure and a global addressing system.

4.2.1 Physical Topologies

A "Topology" is the physical layout or shape of a network. Engineers must balance cost, speed, and reliability when choosing a shape.

Bus Topology

All nodes connect to a single, central cable (the "backbone"). It is very cheap and easy to set up, but if the main backbone cable breaks, the entire network goes down.

Ring Topology

Nodes are connected in a closed loop. Data travels in one direction around the ring. It handles heavy traffic well, but adding a new computer means temporarily breaking the ring and shutting down the network.

Star Topology

The most common modern LAN setup. All nodes connect independently to a central Switch or Hub. If one computer's cable breaks, only that computer loses connection. However, if the central Switch fails, everyone loses connection.

Mesh Topology

Every node is connected to every other node. This is the most reliable topology because if one cable breaks, data simply takes a different path. However, it is incredibly expensive and complex to wire physically.

4.2.2 IP Addressing (The Internet's Phone Book)

For data to reach its destination, every device connected to the internet must have a unique identifier. This is called an IP Address (Internet Protocol Address).

IPv4 (The Old Standard)

A 32-bit number usually written as four groups of numbers (e.g., 192.168.1.5). It allows for roughly 4.3 billion unique addresses. Because of smartphones and smart TVs, we officially ran out of IPv4 addresses in 2011!

IPv6 (The Modern Upgrade)

A 128-bit system using letters and numbers (e.g., 2001:0db8:85a3:0000...). It provides 340 undecillion unique addresses—enough to give an IP address to every grain of sand on Earth.

4.2.3 DNS (The Translator)

Humans are bad at memorizing strings of numbers like 142.250.190.46, but computers require numbers to route data. The DNS (Domain Name System) solves this problem.

DNS acts like the "Contacts" app on your phone. When you type google.com into your browser, your computer silently asks a DNS Server, "What is the IP address for google.com?" The DNS server responds with 142.250.190.46, allowing your computer to load the site.

Step-by-Step: How to Run a TraceRoute

Want to see the invisible path your data takes across the country? You can use your computer's built-in terminal to trace the network "hops."

On Windows (Command Prompt):
  1. Click the Start Menu, type cmd, and hit Enter to open the black Command Prompt.
  2. Type exactly this: tracert google.com (or any other website you want).
  3. Press Enter and watch the screen. It will list every single router (hop) your data bounces through to reach Google's servers!
On macOS (Terminal):
  1. Press Cmd + Space to open Spotlight Search.
  2. Type Terminal and hit Enter to open the console.
  3. Type exactly this: traceroute google.com
  4. Press Return to watch the network hops appear line by line.

4.3 Scalability and Reliability Standard 9-12.NI.NCO.1

The internet was originally designed by the military to survive a nuclear attack. As a result, its architecture is built to be nearly indestructible and capable of infinite growth.

4.3.1 Building for Growth (Scalability)

Scalability is the ability of a network to grow seamlessly. Because the internet is fundamentally just a massive collection of individual Routers passing messages to other Routers, you can plug in millions of new smartphones tomorrow and the internet's core architecture won't break. It just updates its routing tables and keeps moving.

4.3.2 Redundancy (The Safety Net)

Why doesn't the internet "go down" completely? The secret is Redundancy (having multiple backup paths) and Fault Tolerance.

If you are downloading a video from a server in New York to your laptop in California, the data might travel through routers in Chicago. If a massive blizzard knocks out the power in Chicago, the internet doesn't fail. The routers instantly detect the dead end and dynamically reroute your video data through Texas instead. It might take 20 milliseconds longer, but the connection survives.

4.3.3 Packets and Protocols

When you send a 10MB photograph to a friend, the internet does not send a massive 10MB chunk of data. Doing so would clog the network pipes.

Instead, your computer uses TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol). It chops your photograph into thousands of tiny, uniform "envelopes" called Packets. Each packet gets a label with the destination IP Address and a sequence number (e.g., "Packet 4 of 500").

These packets are fired into the internet. They might all take entirely different routes (redundancy!) to get to your friend. When they arrive, your friend's computer uses the sequence numbers to reassemble the tiny packets back into the complete photograph.
